Specific Heat Capacity

Understanding temperature changes and energy transfer in materials

Learn

Learning Objective: Calculate the amount of energy stored in or released from a system as its temperature changes using specific heat capacity.

What is Specific Heat Capacity?

Different materials need different amounts of energy to heat up. Specific heat capacity tells us how much energy is needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C.

Definition: Specific heat capacity is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of a substance by one degree Celsius.

The Formula

ΔE = m c Δθ
ΔE = change in thermal energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
c = specific heat capacity (J/kg°C)
Δθ = temperature change (°C)
Remember: The Greek letter Δ (delta) means "change in". So Δθ means the change in temperature (final temperature - initial temperature).

Common Specific Heat Capacities

Different materials have different specific heat capacities:

Material Specific Heat Capacity (J/kg°C)
Water 4200
Aluminium 900
Copper 385
Iron/Steel 450
Air 1000
Important: Water has a very high specific heat capacity (4200 J/kg°C). This means water takes a lot of energy to heat up, but also holds heat well. This is why water is used in central heating systems and why coastal areas have milder climates.

Worked Examples

Example 1: How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water from 20°C to 100°C? (c for water = 4200 J/kg°C)

Solution:
First, find the temperature change: Δθ = 100 - 20 = 80°C

ΔE = m c Δθ
ΔE = 2 × 4200 × 80
ΔE = 672,000 J or 672 kJ

Example 2: A 0.5 kg block of aluminium is heated and its temperature rises by 50°C. How much energy was transferred to it? (c for aluminium = 900 J/kg°C)

Solution:
ΔE = m c Δθ
ΔE = 0.5 × 900 × 50
ΔE = 22,500 J or 22.5 kJ

Example 3: 50,000 J of energy is supplied to 1 kg of copper. Calculate the temperature rise. (c for copper = 385 J/kg°C)

Solution:
ΔE = m c Δθ
50,000 = 1 × 385 × Δθ
50,000 = 385Δθ
Δθ = 50,000 ÷ 385
Δθ = 130°C (approximately)

Why Does This Matter?

Central Heating Systems

Water is used in central heating because its high specific heat capacity means it can carry a lot of energy from the boiler to radiators throughout the house.

Cooking

Metals with low specific heat capacity (like copper) heat up quickly, which is why copper-bottomed pans are popular - they respond quickly to temperature changes.

Climate

Coastal areas have milder climates because water in the sea has a high specific heat capacity - it takes a long time to heat up in summer and a long time to cool down in winter, moderating the temperature.

Practical Investigation

In the required practical, you would:

  • Measure the mass of a material (like a metal block)
  • Heat it with a known amount of electrical energy
  • Measure the temperature change
  • Use the formula rearranged to calculate c: c = ΔE ÷ (m × Δθ)
