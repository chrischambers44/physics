Changes in Energy

Calculating kinetic, elastic potential, and gravitational potential energy

Learn

Learning Objectives: Calculate the amount of energy associated with a moving object, a stretched spring, and an object raised above ground level.

Three Key Energy Calculations

There are three important equations you need to know for calculating energy stored in different ways:

1. Kinetic Energy (Moving Objects)

Kinetic energy is the energy stored in moving objects. The faster something moves, or the more massive it is, the more kinetic energy it has.

Ek = ½ m v²
Ek = kinetic energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
v = speed (m/s)
Important: Notice that speed is squared in this equation. This means if you double the speed, you quadruple the kinetic energy!
Example 1: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 1200 kg car travelling at 15 m/s.

Solution:
Ek = ½ m v²
Ek = ½ × 1200 × 15²
Ek = ½ × 1200 × 225
Ek = 135,000 J or 135 kJ

Example 2: A 0.5 kg ball has 20 J of kinetic energy. Calculate its speed.

Solution:
Ek = ½ m v²
20 = ½ × 0.5 × v²
20 = 0.25 × v²
v² = 20 ÷ 0.25 = 80
v = √80 = 8.9 m/s

2. Elastic Potential Energy (Stretched Springs)

When you stretch or compress a spring, you store elastic potential energy in it. This assumes the spring hasn't been stretched beyond its limit of proportionality.

Ee = ½ k e²
Ee = elastic potential energy (J)
k = spring constant (N/m)
e = extension (m)
Remember: Extension is how much longer the spring is compared to its original length. The extension is also squared in this equation.
Example 3: A spring with spring constant 40 N/m is stretched by 0.15 m. Calculate the elastic potential energy stored.

Solution:
Ee = ½ k e²
Ee = ½ × 40 × 0.15²
Ee = ½ × 40 × 0.0225
Ee = 0.45 J

3. Gravitational Potential Energy (Raised Objects)

When you lift an object up, you give it gravitational potential energy. The higher you lift it, the more GPE it gains.

Ep = m g h
Ep = gravitational potential energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
g = gravitational field strength (N/kg)
h = height (m)
On Earth: The gravitational field strength (g) is approximately 9.8 N/kg (or 10 N/kg for easier calculations). This will be given in exam questions.
Example 4: Calculate the GPE gained by a 60 kg person climbing a 3 m ladder. (g = 10 N/kg)

Solution:
Ep = m g h
Ep = 60 × 10 × 3
Ep = 1800 J or 1.8 kJ

Example 5: A 2 kg book on a shelf has 50 J of GPE. How high is the shelf? (g = 10 N/kg)

Solution:
Ep = m g h
50 = 2 × 10 × h
50 = 20h
h = 50 ÷ 20 = 2.5 m

Energy Transfers

These three types of energy often transfer between each other:

Roller Coaster:
  • At the top: Maximum GPE, minimum kinetic energy
  • Going down: GPE transfers to kinetic energy
  • At the bottom: Minimum GPE, maximum kinetic energy
  • Some energy is always "wasted" as thermal energy due to friction and air resistance
Bungee Jump:
  • At the top: Maximum GPE
  • Falling: GPE → kinetic energy
  • When rope tightens: Kinetic energy → elastic potential energy
  • Bouncing back up: Elastic potential energy → kinetic energy → GPE

QUESTION
What is the formula for kinetic energy?
ANSWER
Ek = ½ m v² (or Ek = 0.5 × mass × speed²)
QUESTION
What happens to kinetic energy if you double the speed?
ANSWER
It quadruples (becomes 4 times bigger) because speed is squared in the equation
QUESTION
What is the formula for elastic potential energy?
ANSWER
Ee = ½ k e² (or Ee = 0.5 × spring constant × extension²)
QUESTION
What is the formula for gravitational potential energy?
ANSWER
Ep = m g h (or GPE = mass × g × height)
QUESTION
What is the value of g (gravitational field strength) on Earth?
ANSWER
Approximately 9.8 N/kg (often rounded to 10 N/kg in calculations)
QUESTION
On a roller coaster, when is gravitational potential energy at maximum?
ANSWER
At the highest point of the track
