Energy Transfers and Efficiency

Understanding useful and wasted energy

Learn

Learning Objectives: Describe how energy is transferred and dissipated in systems. Calculate efficiency and explain ways to reduce unwanted energy transfers.

Conservation of Energy

The Golden Rule: Energy can be transferred usefully, stored or dissipated, but it cannot be created or destroyed. The total amount of energy in a closed system always stays the same.

In any system change, energy is transferred from one store to another, but some energy is always dissipated (spread out) to the surroundings in ways that are not useful. This dissipated energy is often called "wasted" energy.

Useful and Wasted Energy

Example: A Light Bulb
  • Input: Electrical energy
  • Useful output: Light energy (what we want)
  • Wasted output: Thermal energy (heat - bulb gets hot)
Example: A Car Engine
  • Input: Chemical energy (fuel)
  • Useful output: Kinetic energy (movement)
  • Wasted output: Thermal energy (engine heat), sound energy (noise)

What is Efficiency?

Efficiency tells us how good a device is at transferring energy into useful forms. The more efficient something is, the less energy is wasted.

Efficiency = Useful output energy / Total input energy
Or as a percentage:
Efficiency (%) = (Useful output energy / Total input energy) × 100
Efficiency = Useful power output / Total power input
Or as a percentage:
Efficiency (%) = (Useful power output / Total power input) × 100
Remember: Efficiency has no units when given as a decimal (e.g., 0.75) but can be written as a percentage (e.g., 75%). Efficiency can never be more than 1 (or 100%).

Worked Examples

Example 1: A motor transfers 1000 J of electrical energy. 750 J is transferred as useful kinetic energy. Calculate the efficiency.

Solution:
Efficiency = useful output / total input
Efficiency = 750 / 1000
Efficiency = 0.75 or 75%

Example 2: An electric heater has a power input of 2000 W. It transfers 1900 W usefully as heat. Calculate its efficiency.

Solution:
Efficiency = useful power out / total power in
Efficiency = 1900 / 2000
Efficiency = 0.95 or 95%

Example 3: A lamp has an efficiency of 20%. If 100 J of electrical energy is supplied, how much useful light energy is produced?

Solution:
Efficiency = useful output / total input
0.2 = useful output / 100
Useful output = 0.2 × 100 = 20 J

Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

Lubrication

Oil or grease between moving parts reduces friction, which reduces energy wasted as heat and sound. This is why we oil bike chains and car engines.

Thermal Insulation

Materials like foam, fibreglass, or air gaps reduce energy transfer by heating. Double glazing in windows traps air between two panes of glass, reducing heat loss from homes.

Streamlining

Smooth, aerodynamic shapes reduce air resistance, meaning less energy is wasted overcoming drag. This is why cars, planes, and cyclists are streamlined.

Thermal Conductivity

Different materials conduct heat at different rates:

Thermal Conductivity: The higher the thermal conductivity of a material, the higher the rate of energy transfer by conduction across the material.
  • High thermal conductivity: Metals (copper, aluminium) - good conductors, transfer heat quickly
  • Low thermal conductivity: Insulators (wood, plastic, air) - poor conductors, transfer heat slowly
Building Insulation:

Thicker walls and materials with lower thermal conductivity reduce the rate of cooling of a building, saving energy on heating.

Higher Tier: Increasing Efficiency

Ways to increase efficiency of intended energy transfers:

  • Reduce friction (lubrication, smoother surfaces)
  • Reduce air resistance (streamlining)
  • Reduce thermal energy losses (insulation)
  • Reduce electrical resistance (better conductors, cooling)
  • Recycle waste energy (heat exchangers)

QUESTION
Can energy be created or destroyed?
ANSWER
No - energy is always conserved. It can only be transferred or stored.
QUESTION
What is meant by "wasted" energy?
ANSWER
Energy that is dissipated (spread out) to the surroundings in forms that are not useful
QUESTION
What is the formula for efficiency?
ANSWER
Efficiency = useful output energy ÷ total input energy (or useful power out ÷ total power in)
QUESTION
Can efficiency ever be greater than 100%?
ANSWER
No - efficiency can never exceed 100% (or 1.0) because you can't get more energy out than you put in
QUESTION
How does lubrication reduce wasted energy?
ANSWER
Lubrication reduces friction between moving parts, reducing energy wasted as heat and sound
QUESTION
What does thermal conductivity measure?
ANSWER
How quickly heat energy transfers through a material - high thermal conductivity means fast heat transfer
