Resistors and Components

Understanding different types of resistors and their behaviors

Learn

Learning Objectives: Understand how the resistance of components like LDRs, thermistors, and diodes changes with conditions, and explain their applications.

Fixed Resistors

A fixed resistor has a constant resistance that doesn't change. Its current-voltage (I-V) graph is a straight line through the origin - this shows it obeys Ohm's Law.

Remember: For a fixed resistor at constant temperature, doubling the voltage doubles the current. The resistance stays the same.

Variable Resistors

Unlike fixed resistors, some components have resistances that change based on conditions. These are very useful in electronic circuits.

🌡️ Thermistor (Temperature-Dependent Resistor)

What it is: A resistor whose resistance changes with temperature.

How it behaves: As temperature increases, the resistance of a thermistor DECREASES.

Why? When the thermistor gets hotter, more electrons are released and can move freely, making it easier for current to flow. Less resistance!

Applications:
  • Thermostats in heating systems - switch the heating on when it gets cold
  • Temperature sensors in ovens and fridges
  • Car engine temperature monitoring
  • Fire alarms - detect temperature rise
💡 LDR (Light-Dependent Resistor)

What it is: A resistor whose resistance changes with light intensity.

How it behaves: As light intensity increases, the resistance of an LDR DECREASES.

Why? When light shines on the LDR, it gives energy to electrons, freeing them to move. More free electrons means better conductivity and lower resistance.

Applications:
  • Automatic street lights - turn on when it gets dark
  • Camera light meters - adjust exposure settings
  • Security lights - detect when someone blocks the light
  • Automatic curtains - close when it gets bright
▷| Diode

What it is: A component that only allows current to flow in one direction.

How it behaves:
  • Forward direction: Very low resistance - current flows easily
  • Reverse direction: Very high resistance - no current flows

The threshold voltage: A diode needs a certain minimum voltage (typically about 0.6V for silicon diodes) before it will conduct in the forward direction.

Applications:
  • Converting AC to DC in power supplies (rectification)
  • Protecting circuits from reverse current damage
  • LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes) - emit light when current flows forward
  • Solar panels - prevent current flowing back at night

I-V Characteristics

An I-V (current-voltage) graph shows how the current through a component changes as you change the voltage across it. Different components have different characteristic graphs:

Fixed Resistor: Straight line through the origin - current directly proportional to voltage (obeys Ohm's Law)
Filament Lamp: Curved line - resistance increases as the lamp gets hotter, so the graph curves (doesn't obey Ohm's Law)
Diode: Flat along the negative voltage axis (no current in reverse), then steep rise once forward threshold voltage is reached

Practical Investigation

You need to be able to investigate how the resistance of different components changes. For thermistors and LDRs, you would:

  • Set up a circuit to measure current and voltage
  • Change the condition (temperature for thermistor, light for LDR)
  • Measure current and voltage at each condition
  • Calculate resistance using R = V/I
  • Plot a graph of resistance against temperature/light intensity
