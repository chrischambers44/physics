Potential Difference and Resistance

Understanding voltage, resistance, and Ohm's Law

Learn

Learning Objectives: Understand potential difference (voltage) and resistance, apply Ohm's Law, and calculate current, voltage and resistance in circuits.

What is Potential Difference?

Potential difference (also called voltage) is the energy transferred per unit charge passed.

Definition: Potential difference across a component is the work done (energy transferred) per coulomb of charge that passes through the component.
V = W/Q
V = potential difference (volts, V)
W = work done / energy transferred (joules, J)
Q = charge (coulombs, C)

Potential difference is measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter. A voltmeter must be connected in parallel (across) the component.

Remember: 1 volt means 1 joule of energy is transferred per coulomb of charge.

What is Resistance?

Resistance is a measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a component. The higher the resistance, the harder it is for current to flow.

Definition: The resistance of a component is a measure of the opposition to current flow.

Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω). The symbol for an ohm is the Greek letter omega.

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in a circuit.

V = I R
V = potential difference (volts, V)
I = current (amperes, A)
R = resistance (ohms, Ω)
Remember the Triangle: Cover up the one you want to find:
  • V = I × R (voltage = current × resistance)
  • I = V / R (current = voltage ÷ resistance)
  • R = V / I (resistance = voltage ÷ current)

Worked Examples

Example 1: A lamp has a resistance of 6 Ω and a current of 2 A flowing through it. Calculate the potential difference across the lamp.

Solution:
V = I R
V = 2 × 6
V = 12 V

Example 2: A 9 V battery is connected to a resistor with resistance 3 Ω. Calculate the current.

Solution:
V = I R, so I = V/R
I = 9 ÷ 3
I = 3 A

Example 3: A component has a potential difference of 12 V across it and a current of 0.5 A flowing through it. Calculate its resistance.

Solution:
V = I R, so R = V/I
R = 12 ÷ 0.5
R = 24 Ω

Example 4: 30 J of energy is transferred when 5 C of charge passes through a lamp. Calculate the potential difference across the lamp.

Solution:
V = W/Q
V = 30 ÷ 5
V = 6 V

Ohmic Conductors

For an ohmic conductor (at constant temperature), the current through it is directly proportional to the potential difference across it. This means:

  • If you double the voltage, you double the current
  • The resistance stays constant
  • A graph of current vs voltage is a straight line through the origin
Examples of ohmic conductors: Metal wires at constant temperature, fixed resistors

Factors Affecting Resistance

For a wire, resistance depends on:

  • Length: Longer wires have more resistance
  • Thickness (cross-sectional area): Thicker wires have less resistance
  • Material: Different materials have different resistances
  • Temperature: For most conductors, higher temperature means higher resistance
Why does length matter?

Imagine electrons flowing through a wire like people walking through a corridor. A longer corridor means more obstacles to bump into, so more resistance. A wider corridor means people can spread out and flow more easily, so less resistance.
