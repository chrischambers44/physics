Power

Understanding the rate of energy transfer

Learn

Learning Objective: Understand power as the rate of energy transfer and be able to calculate power in different situations.

What is Power?

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred or the rate at which work is done. In simple terms, power tells us how quickly energy is being used or transferred.

Definition: Power is defined as the rate at which energy is transferred or the rate at which work is done.

The Power Equations

There are two main equations for calculating power:

P = E/t
P = power (W - watts)
E = energy transferred (J)
t = time (s)
P = W/t
P = power (W - watts)
W = work done (J)
t = time (s)
Remember: 1 watt (W) = 1 joule per second (J/s). An energy transfer of 1 joule per second is equal to a power of 1 watt.

Comparing Power

Imagine two electric motors both lifting the same weight through the same height:

  • They both do the same amount of work (same energy transfer)
  • But if one does it in 5 seconds and the other takes 10 seconds
  • The faster motor has twice the power!
Example 1: An electric motor transfers 6000 J of energy in 20 seconds. Calculate its power.

Solution:
P = E/t
P = 6000 ÷ 20
P = 300 W

Example 2: A light bulb has a power rating of 60 W. How much energy does it transfer in 5 minutes?

Solution:
First convert 5 minutes to seconds: 5 × 60 = 300 s

P = E/t, so E = P × t
E = 60 × 300
E = 18,000 J or 18 kJ

Example 3: Compare two people climbing stairs: Person A climbs in 30 seconds and Person B climbs in 20 seconds. If they both do 3000 J of work, who has greater power?

Solution:
Person A: P = W/t = 3000 ÷ 30 = 100 W
Person B: P = W/t = 3000 ÷ 20 = 150 W

Person B has greater power because they do the same work in less time.

Power Ratings

Electrical appliances have power ratings that tell you how much energy they transfer per second:

  • LED light bulb: 10 W (very efficient)
  • Old incandescent bulb: 60-100 W
  • Laptop computer: 50-100 W
  • Kettle: 2000-3000 W (2-3 kW)
  • Electric oven: 2000-5000 W (2-5 kW)
  • Car engine: 50,000-150,000 W (50-150 kW)
Remember: 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 watts (W)

Why Power Matters

Choosing Appliances:

Higher power appliances work faster but cost more to run. A 3 kW kettle boils water faster than a 2 kW kettle, but uses energy at a higher rate.

Energy Bills:

Your electricity bill is based on energy used (measured in kilowatt-hours, kWh), which depends on both the power of appliances and how long you use them for.
